## 2) Thesis-Hypothesis Connection

### Primary Research Question (Current)
In multi-site synthetic SDN topologies, do AI-driven controller placement methods outperform baseline heuristics on latency while remaining computationally efficient and reliable?

### Thesis Statement (Argument, Not Just Fact)
AI-driven controller placement should only be considered superior in multi-site SDN when its latency gains remain defensible after accounting for compute cost and scale sensitivity; otherwise, classical heuristics remain the more methodologically credible choice.

### Building Blocks: Sub-Questions -> Thesis Test

1. Performance Sub-Question
- Question: Do AI methods achieve materially lower controller-switch latency than baselines?
- Main evidence: avg_latency, avg_latency_mean, latency_gain_vs_random_pct.
- Decision value: tests whether AI provides real network-quality benefit.

2. Efficiency Sub-Question
- Question: Are any latency gains still worthwhile after runtime and convergence burden are included?
- Main evidence: runtime_ms, iterations_budget, iterations_to_converge_mean, efficiency_score, efficiency_rank, Pareto frontier status.
- Decision value: prevents one-metric conclusions and tests practical viability.

3. Scalability Sub-Question
- Question: Do method rankings remain stable across topology family and node scale, or do they shift with context?
- Main evidence: factorial dimensions (topology_model, node_count, controller_budget), cross-scenario rank changes, seed-stability checks.
- Decision value: tests external validity and guards against over-generalizing single-scenario wins.

Thesis logic:
- Supported when AI wins are consistent across Performance + Efficiency + Scalability.
- Weakened when AI wins only on latency but fails efficiency or breaks under scale/topology shifts.

## 3) Credibility and Methodological Rigor

### Implemented Controls in the Codebase

1. Warm-up runs to reduce cold-start bias
- scripts/run_factorial_latency_cost_matrix.py includes --warmup-runs with unrecorded executions before measured trials.
- Purpose: removes one-time startup overhead distortion from runtime comparisons.

2. Deterministic seeding at scenario and trial levels
- Deterministic topology_seed, warmup_seed, and trial_seed formulas are used.
- Purpose: allows exact reruns and fair method comparisons under identical random conditions.

3. Deterministic synthetic topology generation
- topology/synthetic_topology_models.py uses fixed seed defaults for both Barabasi-Albert and Waxman generation.
- Waxman regeneration attempts use deterministic attempt_seed progression to maintain reproducible connected graphs.

4. Repeated trials per scenario
- Factorial runner uses multiple trials per scenario (default 5).
- Purpose: reduces sensitivity to single-run noise and improves estimate stability.

5. Seed traceability in output rows
- Generated rows include topology_seed and trial_seed in the recorded dataset.
- Purpose: enables full audit trail and replication verification.

### Why These Steps Increase Reliability of Primary Data

- Internal validity improves because runtime and quality differences are less likely to be artifacts of startup effects or random draw luck.
- Reproducibility improves because independent reruns can recreate identical topology/trial conditions.
- Comparative fairness improves because all algorithms face equivalent seeded scenarios.
- Auditability improves because each observation retains seed provenance.
- Statistical credibility improves because repeated trials reduce over-reliance on one-off outcomes.
